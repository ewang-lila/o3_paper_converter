Problem 1 Background: Consider a system of $N$ positive ions (charge $q^+$) and $N$ negative ions (charge $q^-$) within a cuboid domain $\Omega = [0,L_1] \times [0,L_2] \times [0,L_3]$. The system is subject to periodic boundary conditions. The state of the system at time $t$ is given by the $6N$-dimensional vector of all ion positions, $\overline{\mathbf{X}}(t)$. A statistical description of the system is provided by the time-dependent probability density function $p(\overline{\mathbf{x}}, t)$, where $\overline{\mathbf{x}} = [\mathbf{x}_1^+, \dots, \mathbf{x}_N^+, \mathbf{x}_1^-, \dots, \mathbf{x}_N^-]$ is a point in the state space. The macroscopic concentrations of the ions, $c^\pm(\mathbf{x}, t)$, are defined as $N$ times the marginal probability density of a single ion of a given species. Since the ions of a given species are indistinguishable, this can be written as: $$c^\pm\big(\mathbf{x},t\big) = N \int_{ \Omega^{2N-1}} p \Big(\mathbf{x},\widetilde{\mathbf{x}}^\pm_1,t\Big) \, \mbox{d} \widetilde{\mathbf{x}}^\pm_1$$ where $\widetilde{\mathbf{x}}^\pm_1$ denotes the coordinates of all ions except for the first ion of the specified type ($\pm$). The system also contains a fixed, permanent charge density $\varrho_{\mathrm{p}}(\mathbf{x})$. The microscopic electrostatic potential $\widehat{\phi}(\mathbf{x}; \overline{\mathbf{y}})$ at a point $\mathbf{x}$ for a given configuration of ions $\overline{\mathbf{y}}$ is determined by the Poisson equation, accounting for all ions and their periodic images: $$


abla^2 \widehat{\phi}\big(\mathbf{x}; \overline{\mathbf{y}}\big) = -\frac{1}{\varepsilon_0 \, \varepsilon} \sum_{\boldsymbol{\ell}} \left[ \varrho_{\mathrm{p}}(\mathbf{x}\!- \!{\mathbf{L}}_{\boldsymbol{\ell}}) + \sum_{i=1}^{N} \Big( q^+ \, \delta^3 \big(\mathbf{x}\!-\!\mathbf{y}_i^+ \!\!-\! {\mathbf{L}}_{\boldsymbol{\ell}}\big) + q^- \, \delta^3 \big(\mathbf{x}\!-\!\mathbf{y}_i^- \!\!-\! {\mathbf{L}}_{\boldsymbol{\ell}}\big) \Big) \right] $$ Here, $\varepsilon_0$ and $\varepsilon$ are permittivity constants, $\delta^3$ is the three-dimensional Dirac delta function, and the sum is over all integer vectors $\boldsymbol{\ell}=[\ell_1, \ell_2, \ell_3]$ with ${\mathbf{L}}_{\boldsymbol{\ell}} = [\ell_1 L_1, \ell_2 L_2, \ell_3 L_3]$ representing the periodic lattice vectors. The macroscopic, or mean-field, electrostatic potential $\phi(\mathbf{x})$ is defined as the average of this microscopic potential over all possible ion configurations: $$ \phi\big(\mathbf{x}\big) = \int_{\Omega^{2N}} \widehat{\phi}\big(\mathbf{x}; \overline{\mathbf{y}}\big) \, p\big({\overline{\mathbf{y}}},t\big) \, \mbox{d} \overline{\mathbf{y}} $$ Task: Using the definitions provided, derive the macroscopic Poisson equation satisfied by the averaged electrostatic potential $\phi(\mathbf{x})$. Your final expression should relate the Laplacian of $\phi(\mathbf{x})$ to the macroscopic concentrations $c^\pm(\mathbf{x})$ and the permanent charge density $\varrho_{\mathrm{p}}(\mathbf{x})$ within the primary domain $\Omega$. ### Solution: \[\; \nabla^2 \phi\big(\mathbf{x}\big) = -\frac{1}{\varepsilon_0 \,\varepsilon} \Big[ \varrho_{\mathrm{p}}(\mathbf{x}) \,+\, q^+ \, c^+ \big(\mathbf{x}\big) \,+\, q^- \, c^- \big(\mathbf{x}\big) \Big]\!\]

Problem 2 Background: Consider a system of $N$ positive ions and $N$ negative ions in a domain $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^3$, which is periodically repeated throughout space. The state of the system at time $t$ is given by the $6N$-dimensional vector of particle positions $\overline{\mathbf{X}}(t) = [\mathbf{X}_1^+(t), \dots, \mathbf{X}_N^+(t), \mathbf{X}_1^-(t), \dots, \mathbf{X}_N^-(t)]$. The system also contains a fixed, permanent charge density $\varrho_{\mathrm{p}}(\mathbf{x})$. For a given configuration of ions at positions $\overline{\mathbf{y}} = [\mathbf{y}_1^+, \dots, \mathbf{y}_N^+, \mathbf{y}_1^-, \dots, \mathbf{y}_N^-]$, the corresponding electrostatic potential $\widehat{\phi}(\mathbf{x}; \overline{\mathbf{y}})$ is the solution to the Poisson equation for point charges, including all periodic images: $$


abla^2 \widehat{\phi}\big(\mathbf{x}; \overline{\mathbf{y}}\big) = -\frac{1}{\varepsilon_0 \, \varepsilon} \sum_{\boldsymbol{\ell}} \left[ \varrho_{\mathrm{p}}(\mathbf{x}\!- \!{\mathbf{L}}_{\boldsymbol{\ell}}) + \sum_{i=1}^{N} \Big( q^+ \, \delta^3 \big(\mathbf{x}\!-\!\mathbf{y}_i^+ \!\!-\! {\mathbf{L}}_{\boldsymbol{\ell}}\big) + q^- \, \delta^3 \big(\mathbf{x}\!-\!\mathbf{y}_i^- \!\!-\! {\mathbf{L}}_{\boldsymbol{\ell}}\big)\! \Big)\! \right] $$ where $q^\pm$ are the ion charges, $\varepsilon_0$ and $\varepsilon$ are permittivity constants, $\delta^3(\cdot)$ is the three-dimensional Dirac delta function, and the sum is over all integer vectors $\boldsymbol{\ell}=[\ell_1, \ell_2, \ell_3]$ defining the periodic lattice vectors ${\mathbf{L}}_{\boldsymbol{\ell}}$. The stochastic evolution of the system is described by a time-dependent probability density function $p(\overline{\mathbf{y}}, t)$ over the $6N$-dimensional state space. The macroscopic (mean) concentration of positive and negative ions, $c^\pm(\mathbf{x}, t)$, is defined as the sum of the marginal densities for each ion. Assuming the ions of the same species are indistinguishable, this is given by: $$ c^\pm\big(\mathbf{x},t\big) = N \int_{ \Omega^{2N-1}} p \Big(\mathbf{x},\widetilde{\mathbf{y}}^\pm_1,t\Big) \, \mbox{d} \widetilde{\mathbf{y}}^\pm_1 $$ where $\widetilde{\mathbf{y}}^\pm_1$ represents all ion coordinates except for the first ion of the specified type, which is fixed at $\mathbf{x}$. The mean electrostatic potential $\phi(\mathbf{x}, t)$ is defined as the expectation of $\widehat{\phi}(\mathbf{x}; \overline{\mathbf{y}})$ over all possible ion configurations: $$ \phi\big(\mathbf{x}, t\big) = \int_{\Omega^{2N}} \widehat{\phi}\big(\mathbf{x}; \overline{\mathbf{y}}\big) \, p\big({\overline{\mathbf{y}}},t\big) \, \mbox{d} \overline{\mathbf{y}} $$ Task: Starting from the definition of the mean electrostatic potential $\phi(\mathbf{x}, t)$, derive the macroscopic Poisson equation that it satisfies. Your final expression should relate the Laplacian of $\phi$ to the macroscopic concentrations $c^\pm(\mathbf{x}, t)$ and the permanent charge density $\varrho_{\mathrm{p}}(\mathbf{x})$. ### Solution: \[\; \nabla^2 \phi\big(\mathbf{x}\big) = -\frac{1}{\varepsilon_0 \,\varepsilon} \sum_{\boldsymbol{\ell}} \Big[ \varrho_{\mathrm{p}}(\mathbf{x}- {\mathbf{L}}_{\boldsymbol{\ell}}) \,+\, q^+ \, c^+ \big(\mathbf{x} - {\mathbf{L}}_{\boldsymbol{\ell}}\big) \,+\, q^- \, c^- \big(\mathbf{x} - {\mathbf{L}}_{\boldsymbol{\ell}}\big) \Big]\!\]

Problem 3 Background: A multi-resolution simulation method couples a particle-based Brownian Dynamics (BD) model with a continuum Poisson-Nernst-Planck (PNP) model. The computational domain $\Omega$ is divided into a BD region $\Omega_B$ and a PNP region $\Omega_P$. The state of the system at time $t$ is described by the positions of $N_B^\pm(t)$ individual ions in $\Omega_B$ and concentration fields $c^\pm(\mathbf{x}, t)$ for ions in $\Omega_P$. The total number of ions of each species in $\Omega$ is a constant, $N$. At each time step $\Delta t$, the concentration fields $c^\pm(\mathbf{x}, t)$ are evolved to an intermediate state $c^{*,\pm}(\mathbf{x}, t+\Delta t)$ using the PNP equations. Due to diffusion, $c^{*,\pm}$ may be non-zero in a region $\Omega_B \setminus \Omega_P$. The mean number of ions that "spill over" from the PNP domain into this region is calculated as $\mu^\pm(t + \Delta t) = \int_{\Omega_B\setminus\Omega_P} c^{*,\pm}({\mathbf{x}}, t + \Delta t) \, \mbox{d}{\mathbf{x}}$. This quantity is interpreted as the probability of creating a new BD particle. To ensure the total number of particles $N$ is conserved, the updated concentration field in $\Omega_P$ is rescaled by a factor $\gamma^\pm$. This factor depends on whether a new particle is actually created. The number of ions described by the PNP model at time $t$ is $N_P^\pm(t) = N - N_B^\pm(t)$. After the evolution step, the total number of particles represented by the concentration field $c^{*,\pm}$ within the PNP domain $\Omega_P$ is $\int_{\Omega_P} c^{*,\pm}(\mathbf{x}, t+\Delta t) \, \mbox{d}{\mathbf{x}} = N_P^\pm(t) - \mu^\pm(t+\Delta t)$. Task: Based on the principle of particle number conservation, derive the scaling factors $\gamma_{(i)}^\pm$ and $\gamma_{(ii)}^\pm$ that must be applied to the intermediate concentration field $c^{*,\pm}$ in the domain $\Omega_P$. The factor $\gamma_{(i)}^\pm$ corresponds to the case where no new BD particle is created, and $\gamma_{(ii)}^\pm$ corresponds to the case where one new BD particle is created. Express your final answer in terms of $N$, $N_B^\pm(t)$, and $\mu^\pm(t + \Delta t)$. ###

Solution: \[\gamma_{(i)}^\pm = \frac{N - N_B^\pm(t)}{N - N_B^\pm(t) - \mu^\pm(t + \Delta t)}\,, \qquad \gamma_{(ii)}^\pm = \frac{N - N_B^\pm(t)-1}{N - N_B^\pm(t) - \mu^\pm(t + \Delta t)}\]

Problem 1 Background: A model for a cluster of mutually repelling particles in 2D confinement considers the competition between inter-particle repulsion and an external confining potential. The confining potential is generated by $M$ fixed particles, which are uniformly distributed on the perimeter of a circle of radius $R$. The interaction between any two particles (cluster-cluster, or cluster-confining) separated by a distance $d$ is described by a potential energy $u(d) = u_0 d^{-\alpha}$, where $u_0$ is an energy constant and $\alpha$ is a positive exponent. We consider a single particle from the main cluster, which, due to the circular symmetry of the system, is located at a radial distance $r_p$ from the center. To simplify the analysis, we use dimensionless quantities by setting the confinement radius $R=1$ and measuring energy in units of $u_0$. The cluster particle is thus at a dimensionless radial position $\rho = r_p/R$. Let this particle be located on the positive x-axis, with polar coordinates $(\rho, 0)$. The $M$ confining particles are at fixed polar coordinates $(1, 2\pi k/M)$ for $k=1, 2, ..., M$. Task: For the single cluster particle at $(\rho, 0)$, derive the total dimensionless potential energy, $U_C(\rho)$, arising from its interaction with all $M$ confining particles. ###

Solution: \[U_{C}(\rho)=\sum_{k=1}^{M} \left[1 + \rho^2 - 2\rho\cos\left(\frac{2\pi k}{M}\right)\right]^{-\alpha/2}\]

Problem 3 Background: Consider a two-dimensional system of $N$ mutually repelling particles at positions $(X_i, Y_i)$ for $i=1, \dots, N$. The confinement-to-repulsion strength is described by a set of parameters $\lambda_i$, one for each particle. Problem 1 Background: Consider a continuous-time quantum walk on a finite graph. The system is initialized in a state $\ket{\psi_\text{in}}$. At discrete time intervals $\tau, 2\tau, 3\tau, \dots$, a projective measurement is performed to check if the system is in a target state $\ket{\psi_\text{d}}$. For the recurrence problem, the initial and target states are identical, $\ket{\psi_\text{in}} = \ket{\psi_\text{d}}$. Let $F_n$ be the probability that the particle is detected for the first time at the $n$-th measurement (time $n\tau$). For an infinitely long experiment, the mean recurrence time (in units of $\tau$) is an integer, $\langle n \rangle = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n F_n = w$, where $w$ is the number of distinct energy phase factors involved. In a realistic experiment, measurements can only be performed for a finite duration. This is modeled by a restart protocol where the process is stopped if no detection occurs by the $T_R$-th measurement. The conditional mean recurrence time, $\langle n \rangle_{\text{Con}}$, is the mean time to detection, given that detection occurs at or before step $T_R$. It is defined as $\langle n \rangle_{\text{Con}} = \frac{\sum_{n=1}^{T_R} nF_n}{\sum_{n=1}^{T_R} F_n}$. Near a resonance where the mean time transitions from $w$ to $w-1$, the system exhibits a slow-decaying mode. This mode is associated with an eigenvalue of the survival operator, $z_m$, whose modulus approaches unity. In this regime, the first-detection probability for large $n$ can be approximated as $F_n \approx (1-|z_m|^2)^2 |z_m|^{2n}$. Let $|z_m|^2 = 1-\epsilon^2$, where $\epsilon$ is a small parameter that quantifies the deviation from the exact resonance point. Task: In the limit where $T_R \to \infty$ and $\epsilon \to 0$ such that their product $\sigma = T_R \epsilon^2$ remains finite, derive the expression for the conditional mean recurrence time $\langle n \rangle_{\text{Con}}$ as a function of $w$ and $\sigma$. ###

Solution: \[\langle n \rangle_{\text{Con}} = w - (\sigma + 1) e^{-\sigma}\]

Problem 2 Background: Consider a continuous-time quantum walk on a finite graph, governed by a time-independent Hamiltonian $H$. The system is initialized in a specific state $\ket{\psi_{\text{d}}}$. The position of the walker is measured stroboscopically at time intervals $\tau$. The recurrence problem studies the statistics of the first time the walker is detected back in the initial state $\ket{\psi_{\text{d}}}$. The probability of first detection at time $n\tau$ is denoted $F_n$. For such a system, the theoretical mean recurrence time, in units of $\tau$, is an integer winding number, $\langle n \rangle = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n F_n = w$. In a realistic setting, the experiment is performed for a maximum of $T_R$ measurements. If no detection occurs, the process is restarted. This leads to the definition of the unconditional mean restarted hitting time, $\langle n_R \rangle$. It is related to the conditional mean, $\langle n \rangle_{\text{Con}}$, and the total detection probability within $T_R$ attempts, $P_\text{det}(T_R) = \sum_{n=1}^{T_R} F_n$, by the formula: $$ \expval{n_R} = \expval{n}_\text{Con} + \frac{1-P_\text{det}(T_R)}{P_\text{det}(T_R)} T_R $$ In the vicinity of a resonance where the winding number transitions from $w \to w-1$, the variance of the recurrence time, $\text{Var}(n)$, becomes very large. In the limit of large $T_R$ and large $\text{Var}(n)$, the following asymptotic relations hold: $$ \langle n \rangle_{\text{Con}} \approx w - (\sigma + 1)e^{-\sigma} $$ $$ P_\text{det}(T_R) \approx 1 - \frac{\sigma}{T_R} e^{-\sigma} $$ where the dimensionless parameter $\sigma$ is defined as $\sigma = \frac{2T_R}{\text{Var}(n)}$. Task: Using these relations, derive the expression for the unconditional mean restarted hitting time, $\langle n_R \rangle$, as a function of the winding number $w$, the restart time $T_R$, and the variance of the recurrence time $\text{Var}(n)$. ###

Solution: \[\expval{n_R} = w - \exp \left[-2 T_R/\text{Var}(n) \right]\]

Problem 3 Background: Consider a continuous-time quantum walk on a finite linear segment of $L$ sites, described by the tight-binding Hamiltonian $H = -\gamma \sum_{x=1}^{L-1} (\ket{x} \bra{x+1} + \ket{x+1} \bra{x})$, where $\gamma$ is the hopping amplitude. The energy eigenvalues and corresponding eigenvectors are given by $E_k = -2\gamma \cos[k\pi/(L+1)]$ and $\ket{E_k} = \sqrt{2/(L+1)} \sum_{x=1}^L \sin[k\pi x/(L+1)] \ket{x}$ for $k=1, 2, \dots, L$. The system is prepared in the state $\ket{\psi_\text{in}} = \ket{1}$ and is stroboscopically measured every $\tau$ seconds to detect if it has returned to the same site, $\ket{\psi_\text{d}} = \ket{1}$. The experiment is conducted with a restart protocol, where each trial is terminated and restarted if no detection occurs after a maximum of $T_R$ measurements. Near a resonance where two energy phases merge, the mean recurrence time under restart, $\langle n_R \rangle$, can be approximated by the expression: $$ \expval{n_R} = w - \exp \left[ -\lambda T_R (\widetilde{\Delta E \tau })^2 \right] $$ Here, $w$ is the number of distinct energy phases with non-zero overlap with the detection state. The resonance is caused by the merging of phases corresponding to two energies, $E_+$ and $E_-$, and the deviation from resonance is $\widetilde{\Delta E \tau } = (\tau |E_+ - E_-| \mod 2\pi)$. The parameter $\lambda$ is defined as $\lambda = p_+ p_- / (p_+ + p_-)^3$, where $p_\pm = |\langle \psi_\text{d} | E_\pm \rangle|^2$ are the overlaps with the respective energy eigenstates. Task: Consider the resonance formed by the merging of the ground state ($k=1$) and the highest energy state ($k=L$). For a system with a large number of sites $L$, determine the expression for the restarted mean recurrence time $\langle n_R \rangle$ for a quantum walk on this linear segment, where the particle is measured at the end node ($\ket{\psi_\text{d}}=\ket{1}$). Your final expression should depend on $L$, $T_R$, $\gamma$, and $\tau$. ###

Solution: \[\langle n_R \rangle = L - \exp \left[ - \frac{(L+1)^3}{16\pi^2} T_R \left( 4\gamma\tau \cos\left(\frac{\pi}{L+1}\right) \mod 2\pi \right)^2 \right]\]
